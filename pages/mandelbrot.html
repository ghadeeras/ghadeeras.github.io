Mandelbrot

This classic WebGL example renders the Mandelbrot fractal by performing its complex number calculations in the fragment shader (i.e. by the GPU).

As a Muscial Instrument

One of the mouse-bindings could turn the Mandelbrot set into a (unusable) musical instrument :-). By clicking on a point that belongs to the set, the complex sequence that defines the Mandelbrot set will be treated as an audio signal, with the real and imaginary parts fed into the two stereo channels of the dedault output audio device.

That idea came to me as I contemplated this thought: if this fractal exhibits such countless beautiful patterns, does it "sound" as good? We often visualize mathematical concepts, but in many cases we could also "audialize" them. So, how do I go about doing that for Mandelbrot?

The answer was in its mathematical definition. This fractal represents the set of complex numbers such that each number C in it produces a "bounded" sequence:

Zn =
    0, if n = 0
    Zn-12 + C, otherwise

The bound for these sequences is the circle |Z| = 2. If the absolute value of the elements in these sequences cannot explode beyond 2, then they could represent causal, stable, and discrete signals (steady state signals, or attenuating). So, I could just play these sequences as audio signals. It is also known that these sequences have periodic properties given their intimate relation with Julia sets.

It worked! Even though I did not get beautiful sounds in terms of timber, I was pleasantly surprised to find some "order" in these sounds. I think some of the adjacent patterns in this fractal produce minor chords, and the other distances/ratios known in music. Maybe I should not be surprised. After all, tuning methods are rooted in mathematics.
